# Profitability Summary — What Changed, Why, What To Do

**Period:** Jan 2024 – Jun 2025 | **Orders Analyzed:** 5,000

---

## Executive Summary

Gross margin averaged 24.6% across the period — comfortably above the 22% target. However, this headline number masks two significant cost leaks: a persistent defect problem on Hydraulic Valves ($45K in scrap cost, 4.99% defect rate) and a systemic on-time delivery gap (82.5% vs. 95% target) driven by weak schedule adherence (83.4%). Fixing these two issues would improve both margin and customer satisfaction.

---

## Driver 1: Hydraulic Valves — Quality Cost Drain

**What changed:** Hydraulic Valves consistently runs the worst defect rate of any product line at 4.99% — 66% above the 3% target. This generates $45K in scrap cost over the period, the highest of any line, and compresses gross margin to 23.8% (vs. 25.2% for Shaft Components).

**Why:** The product's complex internal geometries create tight-tolerance machining operations where small process drift produces out-of-spec parts. Rework volume is also elevated at 2,349 units, adding labor cost without recovering material.

**What to do:** Deploy Statistical Process Control (SPC) on the three critical valve dimensions. A 1.5-point reduction in defect rate (from 5.0% to 3.5%) would recover approximately $15K in annual scrap cost and lift the line's margin toward 24.5%. Prioritize operator training on MCH machines with the highest reject rates.

---

## Driver 2: On-Time Delivery — Systemic Planning Gap

**What changed:** OTD has averaged 82.5% across the full 18-month period with no sustained improvement trend. The gap to the 95% target is 12.5 points — the largest deviation of any KPI from its benchmark.

**Why:** The root cause is schedule adherence at 83.4% — actual production consistently falls 15-20% short of planned quantities. This means orders are routinely pushed to later dates. Night shift is the weakest performer at 81.7% OTD, suggesting staffing or equipment availability issues during off-hours.

**What to do:** Audit the planning process. Compare planned vs. actual by machine and shift to identify where capacity assumptions are wrong. Consider building safety stock for the top 20% of SKUs by volume. Investigate night shift equipment uptime — if downtime is concentrated there, a preventive maintenance window may be needed.

---

## Driver 3: Shaft Components — Best Practice Model

**What changed:** Shaft Components is the only product line achieving the 3% defect target (2.95% actual). It also posts the highest margin at 25.2% and the lowest scrap cost at $22K.

**Why:** The product benefits from simpler geometry, shorter cycle times (10 min standard vs. 18 min for Hydraulic Valves), and higher material utilization. Operator consistency is also better — the line shows less variance in defect rates across operators.

**What to do:** Document the Shaft Components playbook — setup procedures, inspection checkpoints, and operator training materials — and adapt it for Gear Assemblies (next-simplest geometry, currently at 4.44% defects). This cross-pollination is the lowest-cost quality improvement available.
